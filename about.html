DrinkIQ started from a simple observation: India has a serious drunk driving problem, and the tools to help people make smarter decisions were either non-existent or built for Western contexts that didn't translate here.

01 — The Problem

Drunk Driving in India

India's National Crime Records Bureau consistently reports that alcohol is a factor in a significant proportion of road fatalities. Yet unlike in the UK, US, or Australia, there are almost no free, accessible, India-specific tools to help social drinkers understand when they're over the legal limit of 0.03% BAC.

Most available BAC calculators use drink "units" defined for UK or US standard drinks — completely different from how Indians drink. A 60ml peg of Indian whisky, a 650ml bottle of Kingfisher strong, or a glass of toddy simply don't map to foreign unit systems.

02 — The Science

How the BAC Calculator Works

DrinkIQ uses the Widmark equation, the foundational formula developed by Swedish physician Erik M.P. Widmark in the 1920s and still used in forensic science today.

Widmark Equation
BAC (%) = [A × 100] ÷ [W × r] − [β × t]
A = Total alcohol consumed (grams)
W = Body weight (grams)
r = Widmark factor (0.68 for males, 0.55 for females — accounts for body water distribution)
β = Elimination rate (0.015%/hr average; 0.012%/hr in conservative mode)
t = Hours since drinking began

Alcohol content in grams is calculated from each drink's volume × ABV × 0.789 (the density of ethanol in g/ml). Our drink database uses typical ABV values for Indian and international drinks.

Important limitations: The Widmark equation assumes a simplified model of alcohol distribution. Real BAC is also affected by food intake, hydration, medications, liver health, body fat percentage, genetics, and the rate of drinking. Results can vary significantly from a certified breathalyser. Always treat our output as a rough estimate, never as grounds for deciding to drive.
