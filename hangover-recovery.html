Recovery Guide

Hangover Recovery:
What Actually Helps

A hangover is not one condition. It is the convergence of several physiological processes — each addressable, none quickly. Understanding what is happening inside the body is the first step toward managing it sensibly.

01 — The Biology

What Is Actually Happening

A hangover is a systemic response triggered by alcohol metabolism and its byproducts — not simply the aftermath of intoxication. Five primary mechanisms drive the experience.

01 Dehydration Alcohol suppresses ADH, an antidiuretic hormone, causing the kidneys to excrete more fluid than is consumed. The result is systemic dehydration.
02 Electrolyte Imbalance Increased urination depletes sodium, potassium, and magnesium — contributing to headaches, muscle weakness, and fatigue.
03 Inflammation Alcohol triggers cytokine release — pro-inflammatory signalling molecules associated with nausea, fatigue, and impaired cognition.
04 Blood Sugar Disruption Alcohol inhibits gluconeogenesis in the liver. The resulting hypoglycaemia drives shakiness, weakness, and difficulty concentrating.
05 Sleep Fragmentation Alcohol suppresses REM sleep architecture. Even a full night in bed produces non-restorative sleep, compounding physical and cognitive fatigue.
02 — The Fundamental Limit

There Is No Instant Cure

No supplement, food, beverage, or practice will accelerate alcohol elimination from the body. The liver metabolises ethanol at a fixed biological rate. That rate cannot be meaningfully increased by willpower, hydration, physical activity, or any commercially available product.

Recovery is a function of time, supported — not replaced — by sensible choices. What is possible is addressing the secondary symptoms: dehydration, nutrient depletion, inflammation, and sleep debt. None of this shortens the metabolic process. All of it can reduce how you feel while it proceeds.

The human liver clears approximately 0.015% BAC per hour under typical conditions. This figure varies between individuals based on body composition, liver health, food intake, and genetics. It cannot be meaningfully altered by any external intervention. Time is the only mechanism that reduces BAC.

03 — Evidence-Informed Guidance

Practical Recovery

The following approaches address the identifiable mechanisms of a hangover. None eliminate it. All are consistent with widely accepted clinical guidance on post-alcohol recovery.

  • 01
    Rehydrate With Electrolytes

    Plain water addresses volume depletion but not electrolyte loss. Oral rehydration salts (ORS), electrolyte drinks, or coconut water restore sodium and potassium more effectively. Consume gradually — large quantities at once can worsen nausea.

  • 02
    Eat Light, Simple Foods

    Eggs contain cysteine, which supports acetaldehyde breakdown. Bananas replenish potassium. Clear broth restores sodium. Plain toast raises blood glucose without stressing a sensitive stomach. Eat when appetite allows — forcing large meals is counterproductive.

  • 03
    Ibuprofen for Headache — With Caution

    Ibuprofen is a reasonable option for hangover headache in otherwise healthy adults. Take with food to reduce gastric irritation. Do not exceed standard dosage. Avoid if you have a history of gastric ulcers, kidney issues, or significant ongoing alcohol intake.

  • 04
    Avoid Acetaminophen / Paracetamol

    Acetaminophen is processed by the same hepatic pathway as alcohol. When alcohol remains in the system — or when the liver is under stress from recent heavy consumption — even standard doses carry a meaningful risk of hepatotoxicity.

    Do not take paracetamol until you are confident alcohol has fully cleared your system.
  • 05
    Rest

    Sleep debt from alcohol-disrupted sleep cannot be immediately repaid, but rest reduces the inflammatory burden and allows metabolic clearance to continue without additional physiological stress. If sleep is possible, prioritise it.

04 — Common Misconceptions

What Does Not Work

Several widely practised remedies persist despite the absence of any supporting evidence. Each addresses neither BAC nor the biological processes driving hangover symptoms.

Myth
"Coffee sobers you up."
False Caffeine is a stimulant that masks sedation. It does not alter BAC, accelerate liver metabolism, or restore cognitive function impaired by alcohol. It produces the sensation of alertness while intoxication continues unchanged — a particularly dangerous combination.
Myth
"A cold shower clears your head."
False A cold shower stimulates the sympathetic nervous system, creating temporary alertness. It has no effect on blood alcohol concentration and does not modify the metabolic process in any clinically meaningful way.
Myth
"Hair of the dog works."
False — and counterproductive Consuming alcohol to manage hangover symptoms delays their onset by extending intoxication. It does not treat any underlying mechanism. Symptoms resume — and may intensify — once the additional alcohol clears.
05 — Critical Safety Information

Recognising Alcohol Poisoning

A hangover, however severe, is distinct from alcohol poisoning. Alcohol poisoning is a medical emergency. Confusing the two — or assuming a person will "sleep it off" — can be fatal.

If any of the following are present, call emergency services immediately. Do not wait.

Unconsciousness or unresponsiveness Seizures Slow or irregular breathing Fewer than 8 breaths per minute Blue-tinged or pale skin Vomiting while unconscious Extreme cold to the touch Cannot be woken
Call emergency services immediately — 112 in India, 999 in the UK, 911 in the US.
Place the person in the recovery position. Stay with them until help arrives.
